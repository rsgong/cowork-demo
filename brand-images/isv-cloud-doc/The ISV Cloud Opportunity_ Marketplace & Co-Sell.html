Part One · Marketplace

The conveyor belt
your revenue team
doesn't know it needs.

Every major cloud provider has built a marketplace. Buyers pre-commit cloud spend. Your software can draw down against that commitment. The economics are extraordinary — and almost entirely dependent on whether your sellers know how to use it.

Marketplace is not a listing. It is a buying motion. When a customer pre-commits $10M to a cloud provider, that spend needs to go somewhere. Software purchases transacted through marketplace draw down against that commitment — which means procurement, legal, and finance have already approved the mechanism. The remaining question is whether your sellers know how to route a deal through it.

Most don't. Not because they're unwilling, but because no one has embedded the guidance into the moment of a deal. The buyer's champion often sits three levels removed from the CIO or CTO who controls the committed cloud budget. Helping your champion navigate that internal ask — at the right time, in the right deal — is the activation problem marketplace teams have chronically underestimated.

The infrastructure exists. The GTM motion does not. ISVs that have closed this gap have achieved extraordinary results: not incremental improvement, but order-of-magnitude growth in marketplace-transacted revenue.

"Marketplace is a motion, not a channel. Winning teams operationalize when sellers bring it in — not whether."

Marketplace as managed motion — process over portal

Three mechanics every ISV must operationalize

Committed Spend Routing

Buyers hold pre-committed cloud budgets (MACC, EDP, CUDS) that must be spent. Marketplace draws down against those commitments. Sellers who understand the buyer's budget situation can position marketplace as a procurement accelerant — bypassing months of legal and finance review.

Private Offer Management

Private offers are the commercial vehicle — customized pricing and terms sent directly to a buyer via the marketplace. Managing private offer creation, approval workflows, pricing changes, and status tracking across clouds requires operational infrastructure, not manual effort.

Commission Neutrality

This is the prerequisite most ISVs skip and then wonder why adoption stalls. If sellers lose quota retirement or commission when deals transact through marketplace, they will not route deals through marketplace — regardless of incentives, training, or tooling. Commission neutrality is not optional; it's table stakes.

What does seller activation actually mean? It means that when a rep is qualifying an opportunity in Salesforce, they receive a prompt: this deal has cloud consumption alignment — here is the partner contact, the co-sell value pitch, and the steps to register. Not a training deck. Not a partner manager to call. A contextual action, in the tool they're already in.

This is the delta between ISVs with 15% marketplace attach and ISVs with 90% attach. It is not about incentive structures or seller knowledge alone. It is about removing friction from the moment of decision.

Activation is a three-layer problem: conceptual (sellers must understand why marketplace and co-sell matter for their deals), operational (sellers must know how to submit a referral, create a private offer, or engage a cloud seller), and motivational (sellers must not be penalized for the mechanism). Miss any layer and the motion stalls.

1
Embed guidance in the CRM moment

Seller activation that requires leaving the CRM will not happen at scale. Surface partner recommendations, engagement steps, and marketplace offers in Salesforce and Slack — where reps already live.

2
Match partner to deal, not deal to all partners

Intelligent matching surfaces the right cloud or ecosystem partner for each opportunity based on customer profile, industry, size, and existing cloud footprint — not a generic list of all partners.

3
Close the attribution loop immediately

Sellers need real-time confirmation that attaching a partner — and routing through marketplace — will not affect their commission. Attribution must be transparent and instant, not reconciled at quarter end.

Part Two · Co-Sell

Real leverage.
Honest expectations.

Co-sell with cloud providers amplifies reach, validates credibility, and — when everything aligns — doubles win rates. It also requires being honest about what co-sell actually is, what it is not, and what the conditions for success really are.

The co-sell opportunity is structural, not transactional. When a cloud provider's field sellers recommend or co-sell your product, you gain access to their relationships, their deal intelligence, and their credibility with the buyer. Win rates nearly double: from 22% to 47% for ISVs that have achieved meaningful co-sell activation with cloud field teams.

The mechanism matters: co-sell works because cloud sellers are incented to grow cloud consumption. If your software runs on — or drives consumption of — cloud infrastructure, then selling your software advances the cloud seller's quota. That alignment is the engine. When it's strong, co-sell is a multiplier. When it's weak, co-sell is a reporting exercise.

Consumption alignment is the filter. DevOps tooling, cybersecurity, AI/ML platforms, data and analytics — these naturally drive cloud consumption. Pure SaaS with minimal cloud dependency creates a weaker co-sell dynamic. Prioritize co-sell investment where the consumption story is genuine.

Co-sell — persistent when conditions align

Co-sell is not a relationship. It is a system. And like any system, it only generates output when the inputs are right. Most ISVs invest in co-sell relationships before they've established the inputs. Then they wonder why the field attention doesn't show up.

WorkSpan AI · Cloud Marketplace GTM Playbook

The real conditions. Set them clearly.

Cloud seller attention is scarce

Cloud field sellers manage hundreds of ISV relationships. Active engagement — real deal collaboration, introductions, joint calls — is reserved for partners with strong consumption alignment, referral quality scores, and a track record of closing jointly. Expect earned attention, not assumed attention. Build toward it systematically.

Referral quality drives recommendation scores

Cloud co-sell portals are increasingly algorithmic. ISVs with clean data, high-quality referrals, and strong win rates surface more often in cloud seller workflows. ISVs submitting low-quality referrals — wrong stage, wrong size, no consumption story — sink in the rankings. Quality over volume, always.

Buyer distance is the hidden blocker

The deal champion is often three hops away from the executive who controls committed cloud spend. Your sellers must help champions navigate that internal journey — making the ask to route through marketplace, connecting them to the IT stakeholder who manages cloud commitments. This takes enablement, not just intent.

Don't over-promise field collaboration

Co-sell at its best is a multiplier. But setting expectations that cloud field sellers will proactively bring you into deals — before you've built the referral track record and consumption alignment — sets your alliance team up for disappointment and credibility loss internally. Be honest about the build phase.

What co-sell actually gives you, when you earn it

Access to pipeline you would never find alone. Cloud sellers see buying signals — consumption spikes, architecture reviews, renewal conversations — that your reps don't. When a cloud seller brings your ISV into a deal, they're not just adding a voice; they're opening a door that was previously closed.

Third-party validation at the moment of decision. A cloud provider's recommendation carries a different weight than your own sales pitch. When the account team hears that you're a preferred partner — backed by a referral from the cloud account executive — it changes the buying conversation.

Compressed deal cycles. Co-sold deals move faster. The procurement friction that marketplace removes is compounded by the trust that a cloud seller's involvement creates. Together, they can cut average deal cycles by weeks.

The compounding effect of referral history. Every quality co-sell referral you submit improves your recommendation score. Better scores lead to more proactive engagement from cloud sellers. This flywheel takes time to build — but once turning, it is hard for competitors to replicate.
