The Big Idea

Imagine a musician who
uploaded their album and went home.

They recorded it. They uploaded it to Spotify, Apple Music, every platform. Technically, anyone on earth can find them now.

But they didn't play a show. Didn't post. Didn't pitch to any playlist curators. Didn't tell their fans where to go listen. They just waited to be discovered.

Six months later, their cousin finds them by accident and goes: "Wait — you're on Spotify?!"

Uploading is not a music career. The upload is infrastructure. The career is motion. The artists that break through play shows, build relationships with the people inside the platform, and drive their audience to stream. The algorithm rewards engagement, not existence. You can be technically available to 450 million people and be heard by absolutely no one.

AWS Marketplace has over 10,000 listings. Your prospect is on AWS. Their buyers are on AWS. Their listing is technically accessible to every enterprise buyer who touches AWS.

Most of those listings sound exactly like that musician.

The One Idea That Drives This Whole Play
A listing means your product is available on the platform. A marketplace strategy means your sales team and partner team are actively moving buyers through it. The gap between those two things is exactly where you live.
Why does the gap exist in the first place?

Because listing and selling are owned by different teams. The partner team gets the listing live — that's their job. But the sales team has to actually use it in deals. And no one ever sat the two teams down and said: "Here's how this works when there's a real buyer."

The result: the partner team knows the listing exists. The sales team doesn't know how to bring it into a deal. The buyer wants to transact through marketplace (they have committed cloud spend to burn) and nobody on the other side is ready to help them do it.

That's your door.

The Market

Why this matters right now.

Cloud providers — AWS, Azure, GCP — have invested hundreds of billions of dollars in getting enterprises committed to their platforms. When a company signs an enterprise deal with AWS, they commit to spending a certain amount. Call it their committed cloud spend.

Here's the thing: they have to spend it. It's already allocated. And one of the fastest ways for them to burn that committed spend is to buy software through the marketplace.

So buyers aren't reluctant to buy through marketplace — many of them prefer it because it counts against their committed spend. The demand is real.

The problem isn't the buyer. The problem is that the ISV's sales team doesn't know how to use the marketplace listing to close them.

What is "committed cloud spend" exactly?

When a big enterprise signs a deal with AWS (called an Enterprise Discount Program, or EDP) or Azure, they commit to spending a certain dollar amount over 1–3 years. They get a discount in exchange for the commitment.

The problem: if they don't spend it all, they lose the unused amount. So their finance team is actively looking for ways to consume that spend. Buying software through the cloud marketplace counts against that commitment — which means buying from a marketplace listing is financially easier for these buyers than buying direct.

Bottom line: the buyer may already be motivated to buy through marketplace. The ISV just needs a sales motion that actually facilitates it.

The Contrast

A dead listing looks exactly like a live one — from the outside.

Both have a logo. Both have a description. Both are technically "on" AWS, Azure, or GCP. You can't tell from the listing page alone which one is generating revenue.

But they're completely different on the inside.

The Dead Listing
  • Partner team owns the listing. Sales team doesn't know it exists.
  • No process for a seller to bring it into a deal.
  • Buyers arrive at the listing and have no path forward.
  • Revenue from marketplace is zero or a lucky accident.
  • Partner team can't prove ROI to their CRO.
  • Listing has been live 6+ months. Zero or two reviews.
The Live Strategy
  • Partner team and sales team have a shared motion.
  • Sellers know exactly how to introduce marketplace in a deal.
  • Private offers are created for qualified buyers, not generic pricing.
  • Committed cloud spend is surfaced and used to accelerate close.
  • Marketplace revenue is tracked, attributed, and growing.
  • CRO can answer "what's our marketplace ARR?" in 10 seconds.

The difference isn't technical. It's organizational. It's whether two teams are aligned on a shared motion.

What's a "private offer" and why does it matter?

A standard marketplace listing has a public price. But for enterprise buyers, a private offer lets the ISV create custom pricing for a specific customer — with specific terms, discounts, and structure — that only that buyer sees.

This is how real deals happen on marketplace. The public listing is discovery. The private offer is the close. An ISV with a live strategy knows how to spin up a private offer for a qualified buyer. An ISV with a dead listing doesn't have this motion at all — their sellers don't even know the concept exists.

What does "buyer distance" mean?

Buyer distance is the organizational gap between the person you're selling to and the person who controls the cloud budget. A champion who is close to the CTO or CIO — close to the cloud spend decision — can easily route the purchase through marketplace. A champion who's deep in an operations team may have a long internal path to get there.

Part of the live strategy is helping your champion navigate that distance — understanding who holds the committed cloud spend and how to unlock it for the deal. That's something the partner team and sales team have to figure out together.

BDR Track

Your job: find the musician who's waiting to be discovered.

You're not looking for a random tech company. You're looking for a specific type of company at a specific moment of frustration. They have a listing. They have a partner team. They don't have a sales motion connected to it.

Here's how to find them — and how to open.

Who to target

1
They're an ISV or tech company with a cloud marketplace listing
Check AWS Marketplace, Azure Marketplace, or Google Cloud Marketplace. If they're listed, they've already accepted the premise. You're not selling the concept of marketplace — you're selling the motion.
2
The listing looks quiet
Check: few or no reviews, listed 6+ months ago, no case studies linked, no "private offer" language on the listing page. Low review count is your most reliable dead-listing signal.
3
Partner team exists on LinkedIn, but it's not connected to sales
Search their company on LinkedIn. Find titles like "Cloud Alliance Manager," "AWS Partnership Manager," "Head of Cloud GTM." Then look at their sales team — do they ever mention marketplace? Probably not.
4
Bonus: they're hiring for marketplace roles
A job posting for "Marketplace GTM Manager" or "Cloud Alliances Lead" is someone announcing they know they have a problem. Call before the hire lands — they're thinking about it right now.

How to open

The worst thing you can do is call about WorkSpan. The best thing you can do is ask ONE question that makes them feel understood. You want them to tell you they have the problem — not you telling them they do.

Call opener — option 1
"Hey [name] — quick question. Does your field sales team know how to bring your AWS listing into a deal?"
Call opener — option 2
"I was looking at your listing on AWS — curious, how connected is that to your actual sales process right now?"
Email subject + opener

Noticed you've had a listing on AWS Marketplace for a while. Most partner teams I talk to are in the same spot — the listing is live but the sales team isn't connected to it yet. Curious if that's true for you. Worth 15 minutes?
Remember
You're not opening with a pitch. You're opening with a mirror. Let them recognize themselves in the question. When they pause — that's your signal.
What if they say "our listing is working fine"?

Good. Get curious, not defensive. Ask: "That's great — what does that look like? How are your sellers bringing it into deals?"

If they have a real answer, they're either not your prospect right now, or they have a different problem you can uncover. If they pause or give a vague answer, the listing probably isn't as healthy as they're letting on. Stay curious.

What if they've never heard of WorkSpan?

That's fine — don't lead with the name. Your goal on call one is to qualify the problem, not close a deal. If the problem is real, you'll have time to introduce WorkSpan on a follow-up call with context. A rep who leads with product name on a cold call loses immediately. A rep who leads with a sharp question earns the right to explain what they do.

How to start the conversation

Don't pitch. Discover. Your job on the first call is to help them say the problem out loud — in their own words. The moment they do that, you're their ally, not a vendor.

1
Open with the operational question
"When one of your sellers has a prospect who wants to transact through AWS — what does that process look like right now?" This surfaces the friction without feeling like a sales call.
2
Go to the attribution question
"If your CRO asked you tomorrow what your marketplace-driven ARR is — how easy is that to answer?" Most of them will pause. That pause is everything.
3
Name the gap back to them
Once they've told you about the problem, reflect it: "So the listing exists, but the sales motion isn't connected to it yet — is that right?" When they say yes, you have your champion.
What if they say everything is working fine?

Get specific. Ask: "What does good look like for you? What would great look like?" or "How many deals last quarter closed through marketplace?"

If they can answer those questions confidently with real numbers, you may be in the wrong account. If they get vague, the problem is real — they just haven't been given permission to say it yet. Stay curious and keep digging.

Who else should you be talking to in the account?

Your champion is the Alliance Executive or Head of BizDev. But they'll need allies to make change happen. Keep an eye on:

  • VP of Sales — They care about win rate and deal size. Partner-involved deals are bigger. If they know that, they become a sponsor.
  • Sales Operations — They own the CRM. Any workflow change runs through them. Get them curious early.
  • CRO — Usually the blocker or the accelerator. Your champion needs to be able to walk into the CRO conversation armed with data. Help them do that.
The move that separates good AEs from great ones
When your champion says "our listing does okay but I'm not sure it's driving revenue" — don't pitch. Ask: "Is the problem that buyers aren't finding the listing, or that your sellers aren't bringing it into deals?" That question splits the problem in two. Almost always, they'll say: the sellers. That's your lane.
